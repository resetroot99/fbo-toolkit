Protecting Houses of Worship
and Schools

Active Shooter Prevention, Preparation & Response  ·  A Security Framework for Houses of Worship & Schools

🤝
Pillar 1 · Community
Connect with Law Enforcement
  • Designate a security liaison to maintain ongoing contact with your local police precinct
  • Invite local officers for a walkthrough of your facility — before an incident, not after
  • Request a facility vulnerability assessment through a qualified security professional or local law enforcement
  • Join local faith-community security coalitions — mosques, synagogues, churches, and schools sharing threat information strengthen the whole community
  • Sign up for local emergency alert systems and ensure your building's address is correctly registered with your municipality
  • Establish a pre-incident relationship with local law enforcement — knowing your facility before an event saves critical response time
  • Ensure leadership knows how and when to report suspicious activity to local police
📋
Pillar 2 · Preparedness
Develop an Emergency Action Plan
  • Develop a formal Emergency Action Plan (EAP) — document procedures for evacuation, lockdown, communication, and reunification specific to your facility
  • Identify and clearly mark multiple evacuation routes and rally points outside the building
  • Map lockdown shelter locations: rooms with solid doors, no windows, and heavy furniture
  • Establish an access control system: who enters, how, and when — especially during services and school hours
  • Install or improve CCTV coverage of parking lots, entrances, and perimeter
  • Ensure outdoor lighting covers all entrances and blind spots — improved lighting increases visibility, supports cameras, and helps staff identify suspicious activity
  • Maintain a current staff, volunteer, student, and classroom roster; use sign-in or headcount procedures for events where practical
  • Create a communications tree: who calls 911, who alerts the congregation, who contacts families
🎓
Pillar 3 · Training
Train Regularly. Exercise Your Plan.
  • Active shooter / threat response training — staff, volunteers, and leadership should know exactly what to do in the first 60 seconds
  • Run-Hide-Fight awareness sessions for congregants, parents, teachers, and students tailored to your specific facility layout
  • Tabletop exercises — scenario-based drills with key staff at least twice a year to stress-test your Emergency Action Plan
  • Tactical first aid / Stop the Bleed — designated personnel trained in hemorrhage control; EMS averages 6–9 minutes response time
  • Security awareness for ushers and greeters — the front door is your first line of defense; those staffing it need structured training
  • Children's evacuation and reunification drills — age-appropriate procedures for every classroom with designated adult escorts
  • Threat assessment and recognition — training staff to identify warning signs before an incident occurs
📚 For Centers with Schools or Classrooms
Every classroom should have a lockdown plan, a designated evacuation route, an assigned adult escort, a parent reunification process, and an emergency contact list — reviewed and drilled at the start of each term.
👁
Pillar 4 · Vigilance
Recognize & Report Suspicious Activity

These observable behaviors may indicate surveillance, pre-attack planning, or reconnaissance. Train staff and volunteers to recognize:

Surveillance
Photographing security measures, exits, or staff without apparent reason
Unusual questions
Asking about schedules, attendee counts, or security staffing
Testing security
Attempting to access restricted areas or probing response times
Suspicious vehicles
Vehicles parked for extended periods near entrances; running engines
Hate indicators
Threatening graffiti, flyers, or online posts targeting your community
Abandoned items
Unattended bags, packages, or objects in or near the facility

Report to local law enforcement. In an emergency: call 9-1-1 immediately.

Observe, document, and report. Do not confront suspicious individuals alone unless there is an immediate safety need. Greeters and ushers should be trained to monitor and alert — not intervene.

Pillar 5 · Response
Run · Hide · Fight — Active Shooter Response
A trained, alert individual at the point of entry is your first and most critical layer of defense. Preparation and positioning are what determine outcomes. Every second of readiness counts.
RUN
  • Evacuate immediately if a safe path exists
  • Leave all belongings behind
  • Help others if you can do so safely
  • Warn others as you leave
  • Call 911 when safe — describe shooter(s), location, weapons
  • Do not return until law enforcement clears the scene
When police arrive: keep hands visible, drop any objects, follow all commands, and avoid sudden movements.
HIDE
  • If you can't run, find a room with a locking door
  • Block the door with heavy furniture
  • Stay out of sight — close blinds, turn off lights
  • Silence all phones and devices
  • Spread out — don't huddle in groups
  • Communicate with police via text or window sign
FIGHT
  • Last resort only — when immediate danger is unavoidable
  • Commit fully and act aggressively
  • Improvised weapons: fire extinguisher, chair, scissors
  • Ambush the shooter together as a group
  • Aim to disrupt and disarm
  • Continue defensive action until you can escape, the threat is stopped, or law enforcement arrives
💙
Pillar 6 · Recovery
Recovery: People First
Immediate (Hours–Days)
  • Account for all personnel and visitors
  • Facilitate medical assistance for wounded
  • Cooperate fully with law enforcement
  • Notify families through a single spokesperson
  • Activate Family Assistance Center
  • Establish communications with your community
Long-Term (Weeks–Months)
  • Offer grief counseling and trauma support
  • Enact continuity of operations plan — when and how to reopen
  • Conduct a security review with law enforcement
  • Pursue security grants — funding available at federal and state levels
  • Connect community with mental health resources
🔒
Pillar 7 · Physical Security
Physical Security Measures

A layered physical environment forces delay, creates visibility, and reduces opportunity. Prioritize these hardening measures:

  • Controlled entry points — limit access to a single monitored entrance during services and school hours
  • Perimeter lighting — fully illuminate parking areas, pathways, and all building entrances; eliminate blind spots
  • CCTV coverage — cameras at all exterior doors, parking lots, and interior common areas with recorded storage
  • Door hardening — reinforce classroom and sanctuary doors; install locks operable from inside without a key
  • Panic hardware and safe rooms — designated shelter spaces with solid-core doors and no exterior windows
  • Clear sightlines — trim shrubs near entrances; maintain visibility from inside to outside
  • Visitor management — clearly posted restricted areas; consistent check-in procedures enforced at all times
🛡
Pillar 8 · Digital Security
Cybersecurity & Online Threats

Physical and digital security are inseparable. Schools and houses of worship face growing cyber and online threats that demand the same proactive posture as campus safety.

  • Protect congregant and student data — academic records, health information, and family data are high-value targets; access controls and encrypted storage are essential
  • Secure email and communications — phishing and credential attacks are the most common entry point; staff awareness is the first line of defense
  • Monitor for online threats and hate rhetoric — actively monitor public channels and platforms for threats targeting your organization or leadership
  • Endpoint and network defense — all devices on your network must be protected; unmanaged devices are open doors
  • Digital exploitation awareness — establish secure internal reporting channels for online threats against students, staff, or the organization
  • Cyber incident response plan — a cyber event can disrupt operations and expose families; know your response protocol before it occurs
Key Actions for Your Organization — Where to Start
Campus Assessment
Evaluate entry points, perimeter, sightlines, and facility vulnerabilities with a qualified security professional
Emergency Action Plan
Document written procedures for evacuation, lockdown, communication, and family reunification
Staff & Leadership Training
Active threat response, Run-Hide-Fight, first aid, and security awareness tailored to your facility
Cyber & Data Security
Assess your email, network, devices, and data — implement safeguards appropriate to your size and risk
Online Threat Monitoring
Monitor public channels and social media for emerging threats or hate rhetoric targeting your community
Security Grants
Nonprofit and faith-based security funding is available at federal and state levels — seek a qualified advisor
